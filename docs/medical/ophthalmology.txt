OPHTHALMOLOGY — Department Reference Guide

OVERVIEW
Ophthalmology deals with the anatomy, physiology, and diseases of the eye. Eye symptoms ranging from redness and discharge to sudden vision loss require ophthalmological evaluation.

COMMON CONDITIONS

1. CONJUNCTIVITIS (PINK EYE)
Symptoms: Red eye, discharge (watery in viral, purulent/sticky in bacterial), itching, foreign body sensation, mild photophobia.
Types: Bacterial, viral, allergic.
Urgency: Non-urgent. Bacterial responds to antibiotic eye drops.
Treatment:
  - Bacterial: Chloramphenicol eye drops/ointment or ofloxacin drops for 5-7 days.
  - Viral: Supportive (lubricating drops, cold compresses). Highly contagious.
  - Allergic: Antihistamine drops (olopatadine), avoid allergens.
Triage: Ophthalmology outpatient. If vision affected, urgent review.

2. GLAUCOMA
Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma:
Symptoms: Severe eye pain, headache, nausea/vomiting, halos around lights, sudden vision decrease, red eye, fixed mid-dilated pupil.
Urgency: EMERGENCY — IOP can exceed 60 mmHg, causing permanent vision loss within hours.
Treatment: IV acetazolamide, topical beta-blockers, pilocarpine, analgesics. Urgent laser iridotomy.
Triage: Emergency ophthalmology.

Open-Angle Glaucoma (Chronic):
Symptoms: Gradual painless peripheral vision loss. Often asymptomatic until advanced.
Urgency: Non-urgent. Routine ophthalmology referral.
Treatment: IOP-lowering drops (timolol, latanoprost), laser trabeculoplasty, surgery.
Triage: Ophthalmology outpatient.

3. CATARACT
Symptoms: Gradual blurring of vision, glare from lights especially at night, colors appear faded, double vision in one eye.
Urgency: Non-urgent. Progressive condition.
Treatment: Surgical phacoemulsification with intraocular lens implantation.
Triage: Ophthalmology outpatient — surgical waiting list.

4. RETINAL DETACHMENT
Symptoms: Sudden flashes of light (photopsia), shower of floaters, shadow or curtain across visual field, painless.
Urgency: EMERGENCY — surgical repair required within 24 hours to preserve central vision.
Treatment: Scleral buckling, pneumatic retinopexy, or vitrectomy.
Triage: Emergency ophthalmology immediately.

5. CORNEAL ABRASION / FOREIGN BODY
Symptoms: Sudden eye pain, watering, photophobia, foreign body sensation, reduced vision.
Urgency: Urgent same-day assessment.
Treatment: Remove foreign body under slit lamp, antibiotic drops, cycloplegic if needed, patch for large abrasions.
Triage: Emergency eye unit or same-day ophthalmology.

6. UVEITIS (IRITIS / ANTERIOR UVEITIS)
Symptoms: Red eye, deep aching eye pain, photophobia, blurred vision, small irregular pupil, keratic precipitates.
Urgency: Urgent review within 24-48 hours. Can lead to glaucoma, cataract, and blindness if untreated.
Treatment: Topical corticosteroids (prednisolone acetate), cycloplegics (atropine), treat underlying cause.
Triage: Urgent ophthalmology.

7. DIABETIC RETINOPATHY
Symptoms: Gradual vision loss, floaters, dark spots. May be asymptomatic in early stages.
Urgency: Non-urgent. Annual screening recommended for all diabetic patients.
Stages: Background, pre-proliferative, proliferative, maculopathy.
Treatment: Tight glycemic control, intravitreal anti-VEGF injections (for maculopathy), laser photocoagulation.
Triage: Ophthalmology outpatient — annual diabetic eye check.

8. DRY EYE SYNDROME (KERATOCONJUNCTIVITIS SICCA)
Symptoms: Grittiness, burning, redness, blurred vision (especially with reading/screen use), paradoxical watering.
Urgency: Non-urgent.
Treatment: Artificial tears, lubricating gels at night, warm compresses, omega-3 supplements, punctal plugs.
Triage: Ophthalmology outpatient.

9. STYE (HORDEOLUM) AND CHALAZION
Stye: Acute painful red lump at eyelid margin. Treat with warm compresses, antibiotic ointment.
Chalazion: Chronic non-tender eyelid lump. Warm compresses, may need incision and curettage.
Urgency: Non-urgent.
Triage: Ophthalmology outpatient or GP.

10. CENTRAL RETINAL ARTERY OCCLUSION (CRAO)
Symptoms: Sudden painless complete monocular vision loss. "Cherry red spot" on fundoscopy.
Urgency: EMERGENCY — treat within 90 minutes for any chance of recovery.
Treatment: Ocular massage, IOP-lowering, hyperbaric oxygen, urgent thrombolysis in some centers.
Triage: Emergency ophthalmology immediately.

EMERGENCY EYE CONDITIONS — IMMEDIATE REFERRAL
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma (severe pain + vision loss + nausea)
- Retinal detachment (curtain/shadow + flashes + floaters)
- Central retinal artery/vein occlusion (sudden painless vision loss)
- Chemical eye injury (alkali burns especially — irrigate immediately)
- Penetrating eye injury / globe rupture
- Orbital cellulitis (proptosis + restricted eye movement + fever)

COMMON EYE MEDICATIONS
- Chloramphenicol: broad-spectrum antibiotic drops
- Ofloxacin / Ciprofloxacin: fluoroquinolone antibiotic drops
- Timolol: beta-blocker, lowers IOP
- Latanoprost: prostaglandin analogue, lowers IOP
- Prednisolone acetate: topical corticosteroid
- Atropine / Tropicamide: cycloplegics (pupil dilators)
- Olopatadine: antihistamine drops for allergic conjunctivitis
- Lubricating artificial tears: dry eye

DIAGNOSTIC TOOLS
- Visual acuity testing (Snellen chart)
- Slit-lamp biomicroscopy
- Tonometry (IOP measurement)
- Fundoscopy / Ophthalmoscopy
- OCT (optical coherence tomography): retinal imaging
- Fluorescein angiography: vascular assessment
- Visual field testing (perimetry): glaucoma monitoring

TRIAGE SUMMARY
- Red eye + discharge, no vision change → Ophthalmology outpatient
- Eye pain + headache + nausea + halos → Emergency (angle-closure glaucoma)
- Curtain/shadow across vision → Emergency (retinal detachment)
- Sudden painless total vision loss → Emergency (CRAO/CRVO)
- Gradual vision blur → Ophthalmology outpatient
- Eye infection with systemic signs → Consider orbital cellulitis (Emergency)
